2.1 The Nature of Soft Divergence
The idea of divergence is often associated with explosion — an uncontrolled increase, a force exceeding bounds. But some infinities defy this image. They are quiet, incremental, and structurally coherent.

2.2 Formal Definition
A Threshold Series is defined as a sequence of positive, diminishing terms aₙ, such that:
lim(n→∞) aₙ = 0, but ∑(n=1)^∞ aₙ = ∞
Each term individually vanishes at infinity, yet the total sum grows without bound.

This structure matters because it models processes that grow without appearing to, that change without disruption, and that give rise to form through subtlety.
2.3 Examples of Threshold Series
- Harmonic Series
∑(n=1)^∞ 1/n

One of the slowest known divergent series. Each term shrinks toward zero, yet the sum grows indefinitely.

- Logarithmic Harmonic Series
∑(n=2)^∞ 1/(n ln n)

Even slower - and still divergent. Here, the path to infinity becomes nearly imperceptible.

- Log-Log Harmonic Series
∑(n=2)^∞ 1/(n ln n ln(ln n))

A divergence so slow it feels like stillness - yet the sum still escapes.
These are not accidents of mathematics. They are blueprints of emergence.

## **A.1 Formal Definition and Convergence Theory**

### **A.1.1 Basic Threshold Series Definition**
A **Threshold Series** is defined as:
```
∑_{n=1}^∞ aₙ where aₙ = 1/(n^α (ln n)^β)    (Eq. A.1)
```

**Properties:**
1. **Individual Term Behavior**: `lim_{n→∞} aₙ = 0` (each term vanishes)
2. **Collective Behavior**: `∑_{n=1}^∞ aₙ` may converge or diverge depending on (α, β)
3. **Soft Divergence**: When divergent, growth is slower than polynomial

### **A.1.2 Convergence Theorem (Rigorous Proof)**

**Theorem A.1 (Threshold Series Convergence):**
For the series `∑_{n=2}^∞ 1/(n^α (ln n)^β)`:

1. **If α > 1**: Series converges for all β ∈ ℝ
2. **If α < 1**: Series diverges for all β ∈ ℝ  
3. **If α = 1**:
   - **β > 1**: Series converges
   - **β ≤ 1**: Series diverges (soft divergence)    (Eq. A.2)

**Proof:**

*Case 1 (α > 1):*
Using integral test with ε = α - 1 > 0:
```
∫₂^∞ 1/(x^α (ln x)^β) dx = ∫₂^∞ 1/(x^{1+ε} (ln x)^β) dx    (Eq. A.3)
```
Since x^{1+ε} dominates (ln x)^β for any β, the integral converges.

*Case 2 (α < 1):*
For ε = 1 - α > 0:
```
∫₂^∞ 1/(x^α (ln x)^β) dx = ∫₂^∞ 1/(x^{1-ε} (ln x)^β) dx ≥ ∫₂^∞ 1/x^{1-ε} dx = ∞    (Eq. A.4)
```

*Case 3 (α = 1):*
```
∫₂^∞ 1/(x (ln x)^β) dx    (Eq. A.5)
```

With substitution u = ln x, du = dx/x:
```
∫_{ln 2}^∞ 1/u^β du    (Eq. A.6)
```

This converges if and only if β > 1. ∎

### **A.1.3 Rates of Convergence and Divergence**

For **convergent cases** (α > 1 or α = 1, β > 1):
```
S_N = ∑_{n=2}^N aₙ = S_∞ - O(N^{1-α})  for α > 1    (Eq. A.7)
S_N = ∑_{n=2}^N aₙ = S_∞ - O((ln N)^{1-β})  for α = 1, β > 1    (Eq. A.8)
```

For **divergent cases** (α < 1 or α = 1, β ≤ 1):
```
S_N ∼ N^{1-α}/(1-α)  for α < 1    (Eq. A.9)
S_N ∼ (ln N)^{1-β}/(1-β)  for α = 1, β < 1    (Eq. A.10)
S_N ∼ ln(ln N)  for α = 1, β = 1    (Eq. A.11)
```
